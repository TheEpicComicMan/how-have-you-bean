  1. Why do plants need to exchange gases with the environment?

    2. Plants need to exchange gases with the environment because otherwise they would not be able to go through photosynthesis. If plants do not undergo this important process, they will not have the energy needed to survive. Being autotrophs, they do not, and cannot, rely on others as sources of food. A plant's only hope for needed sugars is itself and without precious gases like CO2, it cannot survive.

  2. Identify the main gas exchange organs in plants? Explain how this process take place?

    3. The main gas exchange organ is the leaf. The leaf has stomata. These are pores on the underside of leaves that allow gases to enter the plant. The gases diffuse into the leaves based on what is needed. Carbon dioxide is needed for photosynthesis which can only happed during the day. Oxygen is needed round the clock for cellular respiration.

  3. Define plant transpiration? What are the two main types of plant transpiration processes? Which of them has a greater volume?

    4. Plant transpiration is the process of water movement through a plant. Stomatal transpiration is the most dominant form of transpiration being responsible for most of the water loss in plants. It accounts for 90-95% of the water transpired from leaves. As the name suggests, the process involves the participation of the stomata, microscopic pores in the epidermis of the leaves. Cuticular transpiration is responsible for the loss of water in plants via the cuticle. Water vapor directly diffuses through the cuticle on leaves and escapes to the atmosphere.

  4. Define stomata? How do these structures participate in plant transpiration?

  5. Identify the elements that compose stomata?

    6. The stomata is the opening where gases like carbon dioxide and oxygen can enter the leaf. The size of the opening is controlled by guard cells that can swell or shrink.

  6. Explain how do plants control the opening and the closing of stomata?

    7. Plants control the opening and closing of stomata through the swelling and shrinking of guard cells surrounding the pore. When the guard cell swells, it forces the pore closed. When the guard cell shrinks, it allows for the pore to be more open.

  10. Are xylem and phloem made of living cells? Explain.

    11. Xylem is made of non-living material while phloem is made of living cells. A large reason for xylem to be made of non-living material is that is must be hollow.

  11. What is root pressure?

    12. Root pressure is the pressure within the cells of a root system that causes sap to rise through a plant stem to the leaves.

  13. What are plant hormones? Give examples.

    14. Plant hormones (aka phytohormones) are chemicals that regulate plant growth. They affect which tissues grow upward and which grow downward, leaf formation, stem growth, fruit development and ripening, plant longevity, and even plant death. Examples include: auxin, gibberellins, ethylene, and cytokinins.

  17. What is the vascular cambium? What is its function?

    18. The vascular cambium is the tissue in between the two other types of vascular tissue. It becomes important in secondary growth of plants (growing wider). It separates the phylum and xylem making copies of each for each side of the cambium.

  18. How do the 3 plant systems work together?

    19. The 3 plant systems (reproductive, vascular, and response) work together to provide the necessary nutrients to reproduce. For example, the vascular and reproductive systems work together when reproductive nutrients like boron are absorbed from the soil by the root system and transported through the xylem until they reach the plants reproductive organs. All of these systems must work together flawlessly in order to ensure another generation of the plant.
